## Related Works

The relationship between targeting quality and efficiency in search ad auctions sits at the intersection of click-through rate (CTR) prediction, auction mechanism design, and user–ad relevance modeling. Across the selected literature, a common thread is that auction outcomes depend critically on how accurately the platform estimates the probability of engagement for each candidate ad in a given context. Early work on *Click Through Rate Prediction for Contextual Advertisment Using Linear Regression* framed ad serving as a prediction problem in which feature extraction and context-aware ranking directly determine which impressions are shown, implicitly linking targeting precision to system revenue. Subsequent research has moved far beyond linear models, yet the core question remains: better estimates of user intent and ad relevance translate into more efficient allocation of impressions in competitive auctions.

A large body of work focuses on improving CTR prediction architectures as the engine of targeting. *Deep CTR Prediction in Display Advertising* demonstrated that convolutional neural networks can learn nonlinear visual features directly from raw pixels, overcoming limitations of logistic regression on handcrafted features. Complementary advances address data sparsity and feature interaction: *An accuracy improving method for advertising click through rate prediction based on enhanced xDeepFM model* augments xDeepFM with multi-head attention and factorization machines to capture higher-order interactions among user history, interests, and target ads. *CTR Prediction with Visual ID Embedding* tackles cold-start targeting by learning visual embeddings for new ads lacking behavioral history, reporting measurable CTR and revenue gains when deployed at scale. Together, these lines suggest that targeting quality improves as models better represent latent user–item affinities—especially under sparsity and class imbalance—though they largely treat auction dynamics as an external evaluation metric rather than a modeling target.

Several clusters extend prediction into generative and multimodal targeting, where the ad itself is optimized for engagement. *CREATER: CTR-driven Advertising Text Generation with Controlled Pre-Training and Contrastive Fine-Tuning* trains text generators with contrastive objectives on A/B test data so that produced copy aligns with observed click preferences. *CTR-Driven Advertising Image Generation with Multimodal Large Language Models* similarly optimizes image backgrounds for CTR via reinforcement learning on multimodal large language models, arguing that aesthetic quality alone is insufficient for online performance. *Predict Click-Through Rates with Deep Interest Network Model in E-commerce Advertising* takes a different angle on targeting quality by activating localized user-interest representations from behavioral sequences on Alibaba's Taobao platform, enabling more tailored ad selection from dynamic user data. These approaches differ in representation (text, image, behavior) but share the assumption that finer-grained matching between user signals and ad content raises predicted engagement.

The auction layer introduces biases and opportunities that pure prediction models may miss. *AIE: Auction Information Enhanced Framework for CTR Prediction in Online Advertising* explicitly incorporates posterior auction signals—market price and bid calibration—to correct auction-induced data bias and improve CTR estimates, with reported gains in eCPM and CTR during large-scale online tests. This work highlights a gap between clusters focused on user–ad relevance and those that model competitive auction context: targeting quality is not only about who sees which ad, but also about how bid landscapes and market prices distort the training signal. Classical contextual CTR models and deep interest networks improve the relevance side of the equation; auction-aware frameworks like AIE address the efficiency side by aligning predictions with realized market outcomes.

Despite substantial progress in CTR modeling, generative ad optimization, and interest-based targeting, the literature reviewed here does not directly isolate *targeting quality* as a measurable construct and trace its causal effect on *auction efficiency* in search settings. Most studies report CTR, AUC, or revenue proxies rather than formal efficiency metrics under alternative targeting policies. A remaining gap for the present problem is therefore an integrated account that links targeting fidelity—whether via prediction accuracy, interest activation, or auction-calibrated estimates—to welfare and revenue outcomes in search ad auctions, especially under competitive bidding and cold-start constraints.
